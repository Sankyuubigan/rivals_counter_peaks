# About the Program

Welcome to Rivals Counter Picks!

This is an overlay for Marvel Rivals that helps you choose counter-picks in real time.

🎮 How to use in-game:
• Hold **TAB** to show the in-game tray with recommendations.
• To move the tray, hold **TAB** and use the **Arrow keys** (Left/Right/Up/Down).
• Press **Alt+G** to open/hide this main settings window at any time.

The overlay automatically reads the enemy team and the map to suggest the best heroes for victory!

This program is designed to help beginners get comfortable in Marvel Rivals. It displays counter-picks for individual heroes or the entire enemy team composition, suggesting the best characters to choose in any given situation. We are always open to your suggestions on how to improve the app!

On the main screen, there is a hero table on the right: here you can select the enemy team composition (from 1 to 6 characters). On the left, a list of recommended counter-pick heroes will appear, sorted by their effectiveness against the selected enemies.

Keep in mind that the program only provides recommendations for effective picks. The outcome of the match will depend on your adaptability and ability to make quick decisions. If you see that your team is struggling and you have the opportunity to switch to a more effective hero, it is better to do so as soon as possible—even at the very beginning of the match.

# About In-Game Matchmaking
Here is a brief overview of how matchmaking works in competitive games, using Marvel Rivals as an example. Often, the system aims to force players toward a 50% win rate.

Over time, neural network algorithms collect comprehensive data about your account: your main role, your most played characters, your playstyle, and even your psychological behavior. While the supposed goal is to create balanced teams, the developers' primary goal is player retention. To keep things "entertaining," the system creates emotional swings. With a forced 50% win rate system, matchmaking doesn't aim for true fairness (which doesn't really exist in these systems); instead, it focuses on which direction your win rate needs to be pushed. Since this is a strictly team-based game, an individual player's impact is often limited.

Much depends on how long you play in a single session. If you play for long hours and are tolerant of losses, the game will cycle you through win streaks and lose streaks—sometimes carrying you, sometimes throwing obstacles in your way. This is exactly where the smurfing problem comes from, as creating a new account is the only way to temporarily bypass the 50% system. However, if your competitive rank isn't that important to you, playing on a single account is perfectly fine.

A helpful tip: sometimes you just need to go offline to reset the neural network's timers. The longer you play without a break while catching lose streaks, and the more often you log off after a loss (rather than after a win, as many players try to end on a high note), the more frequently the algorithm will use you as a stepping stone to give wins to the enemy team. The system begins to view you as a patient player who is willing to endure unfair matchmaking.

It's highly likely that the developers intentionally avoid adding a Role Queue system. The official excuse is that "it would make the game boring," but in reality, the lack of role lock gives algorithms much more control over win rates. For example, to lower a specific team's chances of winning, the system might throw 5 or 6 DPS mains into the same squad. They won't be able to coordinate during the ban phase, the hero select phase, or the game itself, and will inevitably lose. On the other hand, if the algorithm wants to carry a player (for example, a returning player), it will give them a perfect team where everyone gets their main role, resulting in smooth synergy.

An example of bad matchmaking (the so-called "hidden pool"): players who main DPS will be matched with other DPS mains, while tank mains will be matched with other tanks, intentionally starving the team of balance.

If your competitive rank is highly important to you, creating an alt account (a smurf) can help. For a while, the neural networks won't know your main role or hero pool. Additionally, switching between accounts prevents you from playing overly long, uninterrupted sessions on a single profile.

# Tilt
Tilt is a 100% inevitable consequence of playing in competitive modes. Because the matchmaking system intentionally regulates your win rate, you will inevitably face highly skilled opponents every 2 to 3 matches. To prevent the buildup of frustration and toxicity, you need to know when to take a break in Quick Play or Arcade modes. After two consecutive ranked losses, it is highly recommended to take a break to reset both your tilt and the matchmaking algorithms.

Most people prefer ranked mode because they want to prove their worth to themselves and receive positive validation for their skills. Tilt occurs when our expectations are too high and reality fails to meet them. This means your bar is set too high. The solution is simply to lower your expectations by creating a "micro-challenge" for yourself within the current match.

For example, if the matchmaking puts weak random players on your team and sweaty esports-level tryhards on the enemy team, and you realize the game is unwinnable, lower your bar. Set a more modest goal. The main rule is that completing this challenge should still indirectly contribute to winning the game.

A good goal for such a match: try not to die, avoid feeding, and minimize your personal death count as much as possible. If you are playing with friends, you can create a collective micro-challenge, like focusing entirely on protecting each other from dying. This will narrow your team's focus, reduce the pressure of expecting a win, and make the match more engaging.

By constantly setting these localized goals, you can minimize tilt because your concentration will be on the process rather than your rating. Even if you lose, striving to complete challenges that align with winning will help you improve your overall skill and spend your time productively.

* Bad challenge example: "While playing Rocket Raccoon, I won't heal; I will only deal damage." (This moves you further away from winning and ruins the game for your team).
* Good challenge examples: Learn a new hero's mechanics, try a new positioning style, or focus heavily on survivability.
